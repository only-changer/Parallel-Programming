They include the [[Poetic Edda]], the [[Prose Edda]], and [[Heimskringla]], which consists of several sagas. < ref name= \" :5 \" > {{Cite book|last=Mundal|first=Else|title=V\u01eblusp\u00e1 as a source for Old Norse religion in 2019. The Aarhus Mythology Conference|publisher=University of Bergen|year=2019|location=Bergen}} < /ref > \n \n === The Poetic Edda === \n The Poetic Edda consists of several Old Norse poems of unknown authorship that date back to the 13th century. The majority of these poems come from the medieval text [[Codex Regius]], also known as Konungsb\u00f3k. < ref name= \" :6 \" > {{Cite book|last=Crawford|first=Jackson|title=The Poetic Edda : stories of the Norse gods and heroes|publisher=Hackett Publishing Company, Inc|year=2015|isbn=978-1-62466-358-1|location=Chicago|pages=12\u201314, 47\u201358, 68, 95|oclc=905921490}} < /ref > \n \n ==== V\u00f6lusp\u00e1 ==== \n [[V\u00f6lusp\u00e1]], the first poem in the Poetic Edda, provides some of the most complete and accurate depictions of the 12 lesser realms of Asgard, which include [[Breidablik]], [[Valhalla]], and [[\u00der\u00fa\u00f0heimr|Thrudheim]]. < ref name= \" :5 \"  / >  It also describes the [[Yggdrasil]], a mythical tree that connects all Nine Worlds with Asgard located beneath one of its three roots. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / >  Finally, V\u00f6lusp\u00e1 provides a vague description of the location of [[I\u00f0av\u00f6llr]], one of the most common meeting places of Aesir gods. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / > \n \n ==== Gr\u00edmnism\u00e1l ==== \n [[Gr\u00edmnism\u00e1l]] is one of the shorter poems in the Poetic Edda. It contains a brief depiction of [[Bifr\u00f6st]], one of the 12 realms of Asgard that connects it to [[Midgard]]. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / > \n \n === The Prose Edda === \n The Prose Edda, also referred to as the Younger Edda, is often attributed to the 13 th century historian [[Snorri Sturluson]]. As one of the most detailed descriptions of Norse mythology, the Prose Edda provides a thorough history of Asgard and its inhabitants. It consists of four parts: [[Prologue (Prose Edda)|Prologue]], [[Gylfaginning]], [[Sk\u00e1ldskaparm\u00e1l]], and [[H\u00e1ttatal]]. < ref name= \" :7 \" > {{Cite book|last1=Sturluson|first1=Snorri|title=The prose Edda: Norse mythology|last2=Byock|first2=Jesse L.|publisher=Penguin Classics|year=2005|isbn=0-14-044755-5|location=London|pages=30\u201348, 55\u201378, 80\u201383, 93\u201394, 97|oclc=59352542}} < /ref > \n \n ==== Prologue ==== \n In Prologue, Snorri Sturluson shares his interpretation of the [[Skaldic Poetry of the Scandinavian Middle Ages|Skaldic poems]] and legends. His analysis corresponds to modern historians\u2019 belief{{Citation needed|date=May 2021}} that Aesir gods were, in fact, real clans that travelled from the East to northern territories. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  According to Snorri, Asgard represented the town of Troy before Greek warriors overtook it. After the defeat, Trojans moved to northern Europe, where they became a dominant group due to their \u201c''advanced technologies and culture''\u201d. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  Eventually, other tribes began to perceive the Trojans and their leader Tr\u00f3r ([[Thor]] in Old Norse) as gods. < ref name= \" :02 \"  / > \n \n ==== Gylfaginning ==== \n Gylfaginning, the second part of the Prose Edda, contains mythological depictions of world creation, in chronological order. In this section, Snorri establishes the fundamentals of Norse mythology, such as the creation and fortification of Asgard, and introduces the main Aesir gods such as Thor, [[Odin]], and [[Baldr|Baldur]]. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  Gylfaginning also describes [[Ragnar\u00f6k]], an event that would bring destruction to the Nine Worlds and cause their subsequent rebirth. < ref name= \" :8 \" > {{Cite book|last1=Brink|first1=Stefan|title=The Viking world|last2=Price|first2=Neil S.|publisher=Routledge|year=2011|isbn=978-0-415-69262-5|location=London|pages=43, 71|oclc=751805811}} < /ref > \n \n ==== Sk\u00e1ldskaparm\u00e1l ==== \n In Sk\u00e1ldskaparm\u00e1l, Snorri shifts focus to language and the nature of poetry. In the dialogue between the Norse god, [[\u00c6gir|Aegir]], and the Skaldic god, [[Bragi]], it illustrates how various aspects of poetry and nature are intertwined. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  This part of the Prose Edda highlights the war between Aesir and [[Vanir]] gods, including the fortification of Asgard. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / > < sup > , < /sup > < ref name= \" :7 \"  / > \n \n === Heimskringla === \n Heimskringla is a collection of sagas written by Snorri Sturluson that contains accounts on the Swedish and Norwegian king dynasties. The name of the saga comes from kringla heimsins (\u201cthe circle of the world\u201d). < ref name= \" :3 \" > {{Cite book|last=Halink|first=Simon|title=Asgard revisited : old Norse mythology national culture in Iceland, 1820-1918|publisher=University of Groningen|year=2017|isbn=978-94-034-0018-1|location=Groningen|pages=14, 24, 29\u201332, 38|oclc=1153556794}} < /ref > \n \n ==== Ynglinga Saga ==== \n The first saga in the manuscript further develops Snorri's historical interpretation of Old Norse mythos. In the [[Ynglinga saga|Ynglinga Saga]], he rejects his earlier notion of Troy as the historical location of Asgard. < ref name= \" :112 \"  / >  Snorri then provides an overview of Norse kings and their dynasties based on earlier sagas and poems. In his texts, he provides short depictions of Aesir gods, often searching for parallels between them and Norse kings. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / > < sup > , < /sup > < ref name= \" :112 \"  / > \n \n == Realms of Asgard == \n While many sources mention Asgard as consisting of numerous distinct realms, only a handful of sagas provide their descriptions. < ref name= \" :5 \"  / > \n \n === Valhalla === \n Ruled by Odin, [[Valhalla]] is fortified with a golden hall where the souls of mighty warriors arrive after their deaths in battle. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / >  It also serves as a home to [[Valkyrie]]s who oversee the souls of the dead and guide them to Valhalla. As attested in the Poetic Edda, Odin amasses an army, [[einherjar]], for Ragnar\u00f6k, where his warriors are expected to join him in battle. < ref name= \" :3 \"  / >  They train daily against each other to hone their combat skills. However, only half of those who have fallen in combat reach Valhalla. The others arrive at another realm, [[F\u00f3lkvangr]], where the goddess [[Freyja]] resides. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / > \n \n === Bifr\u00f6st === \n [[Bifr\u00f6st]] differs from other realms, as it connects Asgard, the world of gods, with Midgard, the world of people. < ref name= \" :3 \"  / >  In the Prose Edda, Snorri describes it as a rainbow bridge that starts in [[Himinbj\u00f6rg]]. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  The Poetic Edda ultimately predicts its destruction in Ragnar\u00f6k during the attack of the [[Muspelheim]] forces. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / > \n \n === F\u00f3lkvangr === \n [[F\u00f3lkvangr]] is a rarely depicted realm of Asgard. < ref name= \" :5 \"  / >  Besides accepting half of those slain in battles, F\u00f3lkvangr's principal inhabitants include Freyja and her two daughters, [[Gersemi]] and [[Hnoss]]. They reside in the main hall, [[Sessr\u00famnir]], which is decorated with natural ornaments. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / >  Sagas in the Poetic Edda mention F\u00f3lkvangr's rich flora and fauna, which correlates with Freyja's love for nature and wild creatures. < ref name= \" :5 \"  / > \n \n === Himinbj\u00f6rg === \n Located on the border of Asgard, [[Himinbj\u00f6rg]] is home to the god [[Heimdallr]], who watches over Midgard and humanity. < ref name= \" :02 \"  / >  The Poetic Edda depicts Heimdallr as \u201cdrinking fine mead\u201d in Himinbj\u00f6rg while protecting the rainbow bridge, Bifr\u00f6st. When enemies from Muspelheim destroy Bifr\u00f6st, Heimdallr will blow in his horn [[Gjallarhorn]] to announce the beginning of Ragnar\u00f6k. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / > \n \n === Bilsk\u00edrnir === \n According to Gr\u00edmnism\u00e1l, [[Bilskirnir|Bilsk\u00edrnir]] is the largest building and one of the most significant realms of Asgard. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / >  It contains 540 rooms and serves as a residence of Thor, his wife [[Sif]], and their many children. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / >  In the Prose Edda, Snorri predicts the partial destruction of Bilskirnir during the battle between Thor and the [[J\u00f6rmungandr|World Serpent J\u00f6rmungandr]] when Ragnarok comes. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / > \n \n == Construction of Asgard == \n Upon arrival in Asgard, Aesir gods make it their home, as attested by Snorri in the Prose Edda. After counselling with the head of [[M\u00edmir|Mimir]], Odin assigns other gods to rule separate parts of the land and build palaces. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  However, their territories remain open to attacks from enemies, forcing Aesir to protect their lands. < ref name= \" :3 \"  / > \n \n One day, an unnamed [[J\u00f6tunn|giant]], claiming to be a skillful smith, arrives at Asgard on his stallion, [[Sva\u00f0ilfari|Svadilfari]]. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  He offers help in erecting a protective wall around Asgard in a mere three winters. In return for this favour, he asks for the sun, moon, and marriage with Freyja. Despite Freyja's opposition, the gods agree to fulfill his request if he builds a wall in just one winter. < ref name= \" :9 \" > {{Cite journal|last=Fontenrose|first=Joseph|date=1983|title=The Building of the City Walls: Troy and Asgard|journal=The Journal of American Folklore|volume=96|issue=379|pages=53\u201363|doi=10.2307/539834|jstor=539834|issn=0021-8715}} < /ref >  As part of the deal, they guarantee the giant's safety. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / > \n \n As time goes on, the gods grow desperate, as it becomes apparent that the giant will construct the wall on time. To their surprise, his stallion contributes much of the progress, swiftly moving boulders and rocks. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / > < sup > , < /sup > < ref name= \" :9 \"  / >  To preserve Freyja and keep the Sun and Moon, one of the gods, [[Loki]], comes up with a plan. He changes his appearance to that of a mare, and distracts Svadilfari to slow down construction. < ref name= \" :9 \"  / >  Without the help of his stallion, the giant cannot complete his task in time, and Thor breaks his skull with a hammer. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  Several months later, Loki gives birth to an eight-legged stallion, [[Sleipnir]], who later becomes Odin's steed. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / >  Aesir gods later finish the wall and fully fortify Asgard for future battles. < ref name= \" :9 \"  / > \n \n == Asgard in Ragnar\u00f6k == \n Ragnar\u00f6k consists of a series of foretold events that ultimately lead to the destruction and subsequent renewal of the world. < ref name= \" :02 \"  / > \n \n Ragnar\u00f6k begins after the invasion of fire [[J\u00f6tunn|giants]] from Muspelheim, who destroy the Bifr\u00f6st. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / >  This causes Heimdallr to blow the Gjallarhorn, announcing the upcoming doom of gods. Odin swiftly consults with the head of Mimir, who foretells the destruction of Asgard and Odin's death. < ref name= \" :12 \"  / > < sup > , < /sup > < ref name= \" :6 \"  / > \n \n Aesir gods decide to march into battle, gathering their forces on the battlefield [[V\u00edgr\u00ed\u00f0r|Vigrid]] (\u201cPlain Where Battle Surges\u201d). < ref name= \" :2 \" > {{Cite book|last=Byock|first=Jesse L.|title=Viking language|publisher=Jules William Press|year=2013|isbn=978-1-4802-1644-0|location=Oxford|pages=36, 143|oclc=1035197112}} < /ref >  Their enemies, led by the fire giant [[Surtr|Surt]], march through Asgard, destroying many of the palaces and fortifications. < ref name= \" :7 \"  / >  Odin, Thor, Loki, Heimdallr, and other gods, die in the battle. < ref name= \" :8 \"  / >  As the Vigrid grounds become soaking wet with blood, the world is submerged underwater, ending everything that ever existed. < ref name= \" :6 \"  / > \n \n As attested in the V\u00f6lusp\u00e1, after the destruction of the old world, a new one emerges.
